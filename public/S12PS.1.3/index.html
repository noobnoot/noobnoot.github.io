The Atomic Structure

Atoms aren't just science—they are your daily life.

For instance, did you know that nuclear power plants split U-235 atoms (through nuclear fission) to generate massive electricity?

But when this powerful reaction goes uncontrolled, the consequences can be catastrophic, like at the Chernobyl nuclear power plant in 1986.

This disaster revealed both the immense power of atomic energy. But how did we even discover these fundamental building blocks of matter?

History of the Atomic Model

The concept of the atom began with ancient Greek philosophers.

Democritus believed that all matter was made of tiny, indivisible particles called atomos. His idea was rejected by Aristotle, who believed that all matter was composed of four elements: earth, water, air, and fire.

It was not until 1803 that modern atomic model developed.

  1. Solid Sphere Model—John Dalton

    Dalton proposed a model stating that all matter is made of tiny spherical particles called atoms.

    • Atoms are solid, indestructible spheres.
    • Atoms of the same element are identical.

  2. Plum Pudding Model—J. J. Thomson

    In addition to Dalton's model, Thomson introduced the electron.

    • Atoms are made of a positively charged cloud (or “pudding”) with electrons scattered inside.

  3. Nuclear Model—Ernest Rutherford

    Rutherford disproved Thomson's model after his gold-foil experiment.

    • The positive charge (protons) is concentrated at the center (the nucleus), not spread out.
    • Electrons move around the nucleus.

  4. Planetary Model—Niels Bohr's

    Improving on Rutherford's model, Bohr clarified the electron's stable orbit.

    • Electrons move around the nucleus in orbits of fixed sizes and energies.

  5. Quantum Model—Erwin Schrodinger

    Schrodinger further clarified Bohr's model and used quantum mechanics to show:

    • Electrons exist as probability clouds, not set paths.
    • Neutrons were soon discovered by James Chadwick.

The Atomic Structure

An atom consists of a positively charged nucleus (composed of protons and neutrons), surrounded by negatively charged particles called electrons.

  • The number of protons tells the atom's atomic number ($Z$) and element.
  • The number of neutrons tells the element's isotope.
  • The number of electrons is the same as the number of protons.

Also, the sum of protons' and neutrons' masses tells the atom's atomic mass ($A$).

For example, the atoms of Hydrogen, Nitrogen, Neon, Iron, Iodine, and Uranium exhibit the following characteristics.

The average atomic mass ($\overline{A_Z}$) is the weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element. We use the following formula to solve for it:

$\overline{A_Z} = m_1 f_1 + m_2 f_2 + m_3f_3 + \cdots + m_n f_n$

For example, for all Neon atoms,

We can get the average atomic mass of Neon by taking its weighted average:

$$ \begin{align*} \overline{A_Z} = m_1 f_1 + m_2 f_2 + m_3f_3 + \cdots + m_n f_n \\ \overline{A}_{10} = 19.9924(0.9048) + 20.9938(0.0027) + 21.9914(0.0925) \\ \overline{A}_{10} = 20.18 \ \text{u} \end{align*} $$
