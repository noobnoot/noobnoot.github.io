History of the Atomic Model

The concept of the atom began with ancient Greek philosophers.

Democritus believed that all matter was made of tiny, indivisible particles called atomos. His idea was rejected by Aristotle, who believed that all matter was composed of four elements: earth, water, air, and fire.

It was not until 1803 that the modern atomic model was developed.

  1. Solid Sphere Model—John Dalton

    Dalton proposed a model stating that all matter is made of tiny spherical particles called atoms.

    • Atoms are solid, indestructible spheres.
    • Atoms of the same element are identical.

  2. Plum Pudding Model—J. J. Thomson

    In addition to Dalton's model, Thomson introduced the electron.

    • Atoms are made of a positively charged cloud (or “pudding”) with electrons scattered inside.

  3. Nuclear Model—Ernest Rutherford

    Rutherford disproved Thomson's model after his gold-foil experiment.

    • The positive charge (protons) is concentrated at the center (the nucleus), not spread out.
    • Electrons move around the nucleus.

  4. Planetary Model—Niels Bohr's

    Improving on Rutherford's model, Bohr clarified the electron's stable orbit.

    • Electrons move around the nucleus in orbits of fixed sizes and energies.

  5. Quantum Model—Erwin Schrodinger & Werner Heisenberg

    The two further clarified Bohr’s model and used quantum mechanics to show:

    • Electrons exist as probability clouds, not set paths.
    • Neutrons were soon discovered by James Chadwick.

In senior high school chemistry, Bohr’s model is preferred for better visualization and simplicity.

The Atomic Structure

An atom consists of a positively charged nucleus (composed of protons and neutrons), surrounded by negatively charged particles called electrons.

  • Protons tell the atom’s element and atomic number ($Z$).
  • Neutrons tell the element’s isotope (e.g., Ne-20, Ne-21, and Ne-22).
  • Electrons is the same as the number of protons.

The Atomic Mass

The atomic mass of an atom is equal to the sum of protons’ and neutrons’ masses. Since isotopes vary by neutrons, isotopes of the same element have different masses.

For example, for all Neon atoms,

Since atomic mass varies by isotopes, the periodic table only shows the average atomic mass ($\overline{A}_Z$). It is the weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes of that element.

We use the following formula to solve for average atomic mass:

$$\overline{A}_Z = m_1 f_1 + m_2 f_2 + m_3f_3 + \cdots + m_n f_n$$

Solving for Neon’s average atomic mass:

$$\begin{align*} \overline{A}_{10} &= 19.9924(0.9048) + 20.9938(0.0027) + 21.9914(0.0925) \\ \overline{A}_{10} &= 20.18 \ \text{u} \end{align*}$$
