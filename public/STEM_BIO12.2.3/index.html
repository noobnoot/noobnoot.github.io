Introduction

If carbohydrates serve as a quick source of energy and lipids act as energy storage and insulation, proteins take on a far more diverse role. Proteins are the body's builders and workers.

Proteins are unique biomolecules due to Nitrogen (CHO + N).

Proteins are polymers constructed from combinations of amino acids. It is also the most abundant compound in all living organisms.

Proteins also have many functions, including, but not limited to, structure, regulation, sensory, transport, signaling, defense, enzymes, motor, and storage.

In this lesson, you are expected to:

  1. Explain the biochemical process involving proteins and amino acids, describe their structure, types, and functions.

Amino Acids

The presence of nitrogen allows proteins to take the form of complex structures called amino acids.

Amino acids are the monomers of proteins. They are composed of: (a) a carboxyl group, (b) an amino group, and (c) an R-group (side chain) (Figure 2).

The human body contains twenty primary amino acids (Figure 3). We classify them as either polar, nonpolar, or charged.

These amino acids form peptide bonds together to form polymers:

  • Peptides—fewer than 50 amino acids.

    • Oligopeptides—2 to 19 amino acids.
    • Polypeptides—20 to 49 amino acids.
  • Proteins—at least 50 amino acids.

Codons

Codons explain how amino acids are created.

A codon is a sequence of three nitrogen bases in the mRNA corresponding to one specific amino acid (see Figure 4).

During protein synthesis, the DNA code is transcribed into mRNA, which is then read by the ribosome. Each codon instructs the ribosome on what amino acid to append.

The DNA template strand

3' - TAC AAG GAA CGA - 5'

has a coding strand of

5' - ATG TTC CTT GCT - 3'.

The nucleus transcribes this into mRNA to be sent to ribosomes. The ribosomes convert this into

5' - AUG UUC CUU GCU - 3'.

So, the amino-acid chain is: Met—Phe—Leu—Ala..

Types of Proteins

Proteins serve many roles in the body. They are classified based on their function:

  • Structural—strengthen cells, tissues, organs, and more (collagen [Figure 5], fibroin).

  • Transport—moves molecules around the body and into/out of cells (hemoglobin [Figure 6]).

  • Enzymes—ensure catabolic and anabolic chemical reactions occur efficiently (amylase, cellulase, lactase).
  • Regulatory—influences DNA regions during transcription to turn genes on and off (p53, androgen & estrogen receptors).

  • Signaling—allows cells to communicate with each other (insulin [Figure 7], adrenergic receptors).

  • Motor—assists with the movement of cells and their internal and external components (actin and myosin).
  • Sensory—assist in observing changes in an organism's internal and external environment.

  • Defense—helps organisms fight infection, heal damaged tissue, and evade predators (antibodies).

  • Storage—stores nutrients and energy-rich molecules for later use (ovalbumin, placental proteins).
