Here's a short history of the periodic table:

Scientist Contribution
Johann Döbereiner Grouped elements in triads.
John Newlands Proposed Law of Octaves—properties repeated every 8th element
Dmitri Mendeleev Created the first functional periodic table
Henry Moseley Rearranged elements by atomic number, forming the modern periodic table

So, what exactly is the periodic table of elements?

The Periodic Table of Elements arranges elements (by atomic number) to reveal patterns of their properties and characteristics.

The elements can be broken up into metals, metalloids, and nonmetals.

  1. Metals

    Metals are typically shiny, very dense, and have high melting points. Most are ductile (formed into thin wires), malleable (hammered into thin sheets), and good conductors of both heat and electricity.

  2. Nonmetals

    Nonmetals are generally brittle, dull, have low melting points, and are mostly poor conductors of heat and electricity.

  3. Metalloids

    Metalloids (semimetals) have properties of both metals and nonmetals. Metalloids are semiconductors—they conduct electricity better than nonmetals but not as well as metals. They also do not react as easily as metals.
