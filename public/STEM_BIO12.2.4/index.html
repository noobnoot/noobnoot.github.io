Introduction

Just a single change in the DNA code, and red blood cells can twist into sharp, sickle-like shapes. This is what happens in sickle cell anemia, a genetic disorder that makes it hard for blood to carry oxygen because of deformed hemoglobin.

This shows how complex genetic information is in biology. That genetic information is stored and transmitted through nucleic acids.

Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides. They carry the genetic code of life, affecting heredity and guiding how cells develop, function, and reproduce.

Nucleic acids take form as either deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) or ribonucleic acid (RNA).

Nucleotides

The basic building blocks (monomers) of nucleic acids are called nucleotides. Each nucleotide is composed of three parts:

  1. Phosphate group—forms the backbone of the molecule.
  2. Pentose sugar—a five-carbon sugar (deoxyribose or ribose).
  3. Nitrogenous base—contains the coded genetic message.

The nitrogenous bases take the form of adenine (A), guanine (G), cytosine (C), or thymine (T). In RNA, uracil (U) replaces thymine. Adenine pairs with thymine (or uracil in RNA), while guanine pairs with cytosine.

Three consecutive nucleotide bases form a triplet code (or a codon), which codes for a particular amino acid during protein synthesis.

When many nucleotides bond, they form long strands called polynucleotides, which make up DNA or RNA molecules.
