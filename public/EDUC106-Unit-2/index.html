Introduction

Objectives are essential to instructional design as they provide a clear roadmap for designing and delivering curriculum. Thus, teachers must know how to write and classify lesson objectives to help them select appropriate teaching methods, assessment strategies, and use instructional materials. After all, assessing learning outcomes is one of the functions of a teacher.

At the end of this unit, the pre-service teacher (PST) can:

  • formulate appropriate learning objectives and outcomes constructively aligned with national standards.

Standard-Based Assessment

A standard acts as a benchmark for learning that defines what students need to know.

Standards-based assessment lets students know against which criteria you will judge their work, and the standards attached to each of these criteria. It tells students what performance is required and allows you to gain a sense of how your students are doing overall, based on their achievement of the standards.

Constructive Alignment

Constructive alignment by Biggs & Tang (2011) is a principle that you can use to shape your education. Constructive alignment is about purposefully connecting learning objectives, learning activities, and assessments with one another. The goal of this triangular relationship is to make sure all aspects of education work towards the same objective.

K to 12 Assessment Guidelines

In line with implementing the Enhanced Basic Education Act of 2013 (Republic Act No. 10533), the Department of Education adopted the enclosed Policy Guidelines on Classroom Assessment for the K to 12 Basic Education Program (DO No. 8, S. 2015).

At the heart of this assessment framework is the recognition and deliberate consideration of the learners' zone of proximal development (Vygotsky, 1978).

Additionally, ensuring the learners' success in moving from guided to independent display of knowledge, understanding, and skills are features of this framework along with the development of learners' higher-order thinking skills and 21st-century skills.

According to the policy guideline, assessment in the classroom is aimed at helping students perform well with the learning materials. Learning materials comprise of content standards, performance standards, and learning competencies outlined in the curriculum.

  1. Content Standards identify and set the essential knowledge and understanding that should be learned. They cover a specified scope of sequential topics within each learning strand, domain, theme, or component.

    Content standard answers, "What should the learners know?"

  2. Performance Standards describe the abilities and skills that learners are expected to demonstrate with the content standards and integration of 21st-century skills. Knowledge, understanding, and skills are integrated through creation, innovation, and adding value to products/performance during independent work or collaboration.

    Performance standard, in the other hand, answers the following:

    • "What can learners do with their knowledge?"
    • "How well must learners do their work?"
    • "How well do learners use their learning or understanding in different situations?"
    • "How do learners apply their learning or understanding in real-life contexts?"
    • "What tools and measures should learners use to demonstrate their knowledge?"

  3. Learning competencies refer to the knowledge, understanding, skills, and attitudes students must demonstrate in every lesson and learning activity.

Types of Assessment

In a broad sense, we consider assessment into these essential types:

  1. Formative Assessment refers to various methods teachers use to conduct in-process evaluations of student comprehension, learning needs, and academic progress during a lesson, unit, or course.
  2. Summative Assessments are administered at the end of an instructional unit in a course. These assessments evaluate student learning by comparing performance to a standard or benchmark.
  3. Diagnostic Assessment evaluates students' strengths, weaknesses, knowledge, and skills before instruction or at the beginning of a unit, lesson, quarter, or period.
  4. Placement Assessment is usually done at the beginning of the school year to determine what a student, especially a new student, already know in order to test their eligibility or to place them in the right class.
  5. Traditional Assessment refers to standardized testing that uses testing questions with a limited number of answer choices (generally close-ended). It includes multiple choice, true or false, and short responses.
  6. Authentic Assessment refers to integrating learning and assessments to ensure that student's needs are being met. Authentic assessment is where students thoughtfully apply their acquired skills to a new situation or environment.

Competencies, Objectives, Outcomes

Of course, before we devise appropriate targets (i.e., instructional objectives), we must know and differentiate the basic terms concerning this. As stated by Hartel and Foegeding (2004),

  • A competency is a general statement detailing the desired knowledge and skills of students graduating from a subject, course, or program.
  • An objective is a very general (or very specific, e.g., in a lesson plan) statement about the larger goals of the subject, course, or program.
  • An outcome is a very specific statement that describes exactly what a student can do in some measurable way. A competency may have several specific learning outcomes so a course or subject typically contains more outcomes than competencies.

Competency Objective Outcome
M7NS-Ia-2:
Illustrates the union and intersection of sets and the difference of two sets 		Define union, the intersection of sets, and the difference between two sets Use a Venn diagram to illustrate the union, intersection, and difference of two sets

Characteristics of Objectives (SMARTA)

There are many ways to describe the characteristics of a well-written learning objective. One recognized way is the use of the mnemonic SMARTA defined below.

Characteristic Description
Specific This characteristic highlights using clear, non-misleading, direct, and precise language in writing learning objectives.
Measurable Your point and the result of your objective must be written in a manner that can be determined or measured.
Attainable It must be set so that learners can achieve, perform, and satisfy. It must not be too simple or too hard.
Relevant The objective must be one where the learner can see the value of learning the topic. It must yield a purposive result.
Time-Bound We ought to set out appropriate and enough time constraints for the student to fulfill the objective.
Aligned Of course, the objective must be aligned with standards set by the curriculum or national standard.

Learning Domains and Taxonomies

There are three main domains of learning that all teachers should know and use in constructing instructional objectives: cognitive (knowledge), affective (values), and psychomotor (skills) domain.

  • The cognitive domain involves knowledge and the development of intellectual skills. This includes recall and recognition of specific facts, procedural patterns, and concepts that serve in the development of intellectual abilities and skills. Two taxonomies are strongly associated with this domain:

    • Bloom's Taxonomy (Bloom et al., 1964) and
    • Revised Bloom's Taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001).

Other Taxonomies

While Bloom's Taxonomy has been quite useful in extending learning from simply remembering to more complex cognitive structures, fewer models have come along.

  1. One model that might prove more useful is the Structure of Observed Learning Outcome (SOLO) taxonomy (Bigg's & Collis, 1982). It is a model that describes levels of increasing complexity in a learner's understanding of subjects.

    The model consists of five levels in order of understanding.

    Level Description
    Pre-structural The learner doesn't understand the lesson and uses a much too simple means of going about it—the learner is unsure about the lesson or subject.
    Uni-structural The learner's response only focuses on one relevant aspect—the learner has only a basic concept about the subject.
    Multi-structural The learner's response focuses on several relevant aspects but they are treated independently—the learner has several concepts about the subject but they are disconnected. Assessment of this level is primarily quantitative.
    Relational The different aspects have become integrated into a coherent whole—the learner has mastered the complexity of the subject by being able to join all the parts together. This level is what is normally meant by an adequate understanding of the subject.
    Extended abstract The previously integrated whole may be conceptualized at a higher level of abstraction and generalized to a new topic or area—the learner is now able to create new ideas based on their mastery of the subject.

  2. Marzano's A New Taxonomy of Educational Objectives (Marzano & Kendall, 2007) incorporates a wider range of factors that affect how students think. It also provides a more research-based theory to help teachers improve their students' thinking.

    Manzano's taxonomy is made up of three systems the Knowledge Domain working together. The three systems are the Self-System, the Metacognitive System, and the Cognitive System.

    • The Self-System monitors student's behavior, attitudes, and decision-making.
    • The Metacognitive System sets goals and keeps track of how well they are doing an activity.
    • The Cognitive System processes all the necessary information in the learning process.
    • The Knowledge Domain stores information and provides the necessary content.

    To see how these work together, consider the example situation below.

    Currently, Pedro is in his Math class with his teacher discussing the lesson. He hears his classmates talking about the latest episode of the live-action version of One Piece. However, since the lesson is critical for the periodical exam,

    • his Self-System decides to stop thinking about it and engage in the lesson;
    • his Metacognitive System tells him to pay attention and ask questions during the discussion;
    • his Cognitive System aided him in thinking strategies he needs to make sense of the teacher's instructions; and
    • his Knowledge Domain provides him with learned concepts and processes to complete the problems successfully.

    As you can see, each component of the New Taxonomy contributes to Pedro's success at learning the math concepts and skills of the lesson.

Unpacking competencies using 5Ps

We use the term 'unpacking' as a technique teachers can use to make sense of standards and then create focused learning targets to make them actionable. This process, also called “deconstructing” or “unwrapping” standards, fosters collaborative dialogue that supports growth and effectiveness.

  1. Purpose—If a lesson is to be taught, there must be a good reason for teaching it.

    • What are you teaching? Why are you teaching this?
    • Where does it fit into the curriculum/Schemes of Learning?
    • How will it benefit the pupils?

  2. Preparation—Are you ready to deliver the lesson?

    • Do you have the right resources?
    • Is the classroom fit for purpose?
    • Do you need any special arrangements for the lesson?

  3. Pitch—The pitch of the lesson must ensure that all pupils can access the materials.

    • Describe the type and range of differentiation required.
    • Identify the range of ‘Levelness’ and what this looks like.
    • Identify how extensions might be made if necessary (higher or lower).

  4. Pace—The pace must maintain interest and ensure learning.

    • What are the activities for the lesson going to be?
    • How frequently will the activities change?
    • How will the pupils know the time frame for each activity?

  5. Progress—You and the students must know that progress has been made.

    • How will you know that progress has been made?
    • When will pupils refer to and reflect on the Learning Objectives?
    • How do you know progress has been made?

The ABCD of the Statement of Objectives

  • Audience—describes who will achieve your objective
  • Behavior—use action verbs to write observable and measurable behavior that shows mastery of the objective Using words like understand and learn in writing objectives is generally unacceptable as they are difficult to measure.
  • Condition—the circumstances under which the objectives must be completed
  • Degree—describes the level of mastery the student must demonstrate to indicate that the learners successfully mastered your objective

Written objectives are a vital part of instructional design because they provide the roadmap for designing and delivering the curriculum. You can see these components together in the example below:

Legend: Audience, Behavior, Condition, and Degree. (Notice that the order of ABCD may vary.)

Example 1:

Given a bar, line, or circle graph, the seventh-grade mathematics student will verbally present the statistical or numerical information shown on the graph with at least 7 out of 8 (87%) accuracy.

Example 2:

The ninth-grade science student when provided with a copy of the periodic table will describe characteristic properties of assigned groups of elements in at least 7 out of 10 (70%) instances.

Writing Objectives from Competencies

You can use the following steps to write your own learning objectives.

  1. Carefully read and analyze the Content Standards, Performance Standards, and Learning Competencies of your guide.
  2. Look for signal words for knowledge (noun), skills (verbs), and conditions for attaining such standards and competencies.
  3. Analyze and make connections between signal words. It should answer, "What does the standard/competency target?"
  4. Develop the learning objective to attain the signal words—these are the unpacked competencies.
  5. Prioritize as needed for the arrangement of the developed objectives (usually, the higher priority goes first).

Below is a simple example of learning objectives unpacked from the Grade 8 Third Quarter Mathematics Curriculum Guide (2016).

Content Standards Performance Standards Learning Competencies
The learner demonstrates understanding of key concepts of parallelograms and triangle similarity. The learner is able to investigate, analyze, and solve problems involving parallelograms and triangle similarity through appropriate and accurate representation. The learner proves the Pythagorean Theorem.

Notice that the three objectives below may involve a mixture of association from the cognitive, affective, and psychomotor domains.

At the end of the twenty (20) minutes lesson, students should be able to:

  • State and prove the Pythagorean theorem;
  • solve problems involving right triangles; and
  • show enthusiasm for relating the Pythagorean theorem to real-life scenarios.
