What is Physical Science?

Have you ever wondered why things fall, how electricity powers your phone, or what everything around you is made of? That’s exactly what Physical Science helps you explore.

Physical Science is all about understanding the non-living parts of our world—from the tiniest atom to the vastness of space. It's a science that explains how things work and why they happen the way they do.

Physical Science is the branch of natural science that studies matter and energy and their interactions. It combines general knowledge from two major fields:

  • Chemistry–the study of matter, its properties, structure, and how it changes.
  • Physics–the study of forces, motion, energy, and the laws of nature.

The semester's first half will delve deeply into chemistry and the origin of the elements.

Basic Terms

Chemistry is all about matter, which is anything that has mass and occupies space. Chemistry studies the composition and properties of matter, as well as the changes it undergoes.

The following are some key terms important to learn about chemistry:

  1. Atom—the smallest unit of matter. It came from the Greek word “atomos,” which means “indivisible.”

    Atoms are composed of three subatomic particles:

    1. Protons—positively charged particles found in the nucleus. The number of protons defines what kind of element this atom belongs to.
    2. Neutrons—particles of no charge found in the nucleus. The number of neutrons defines what kind of isotope this atom belongs to.
    3. Electrons—tiny, negatively charged particles circling the nuclei.

  2. Element—atoms of the same number of protons (e.g., all atoms with 53 protons are all Iodine).

    • Isotopes—variants of an element but with a different number of neutrons. (e.g., Iodine-127 and Iodine-131.) Some isotopes are stable, while unstable isotopes undergo radioactive decay, releasing energy in the form of radiation.

  3. Molecule—two or more atoms bonded together. They can be of the same element (e.g., O₂, H₂) or not (H₂O, CO₂). If they are not of the same element, we call it a compound.

    • Compounds—special molecules in which atoms are made out of two or more different elements (e.g., H₂O, NaCl, C3H8O).
