Introduction

Carbohydrates like glycogen stored in our bodies are considered a short-term energy source. For longer-term options, we store our energy in fats. And in biochemistry, we collectively call them lipids.

Lipids, from the Greek word “lipos,” meaning “fats,” are non-polymer biological molecules known for their insolubility in water. Similar to carbohydrates, carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen compose lipids.

Besides storing long-term energy, lipids insulate our bodies against the cold and form the plasma membranes of our cells. They also cushion vital organs, act as chemical messengers in the form of hormones, and help the body absorb fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K).

Fatty Acids

Most lipids are built from fatty acids, long chains of carbon and hydrogen with a carboxyl group (–COOH) at one end.

Fatty acids are classified into saturated and unsaturated based on the number of carbon-carbon double bonds (Figure 2).

  • Saturated fats have no double bonds, are solid at room temperature, and are found in butter and animal fat.

  • Unsaturated fats have one (mono) or more (poly) double bonds and are liquid at room temperature.

    • Monounsaturated fats are found in olive, canola, peanut oils, and avocados.
    • Polyunsaturated fats are found in fatty fish, seeds, and nuts.

Classification of Fats

Lipids are divided into five major classifications based on their structure and functions.

  1. Fats and Oils (Triglycerides)

    These are the most abundant of the lipid compounds. At room temperature, fats are solid, while oils are liquid.

    Structure: 1 glycerol + 3 fatty acids (Figure 3).

    Example: butter, lard, fatty meat, coconut oil, and margarine contain saturated fatty acids, while olive oil and fish oils (unprocessed) are generally unsaturated.

  2. Phospholipids

    These lipids form part of the structure of the cell membrane.

    Structure: 1 glycerol + 2 fatty acids

    Example: phosphatidylcholine, which is abundant in eukaryotic cell membranes.

  3. Waxes

    Waxes are harder and less greasy lipids compared to fats.They have a high melting point and are solid at room temperature.

    Structure: long chain of fatty acids joined to alcohols.

    Examples: wax from palm leaves and beeswax.

  4. Prostaglandins

    Prostaglandins are lipids that are not stored but are only produced when needed. They mediate fever, redness, swelling, and pain.

    Structure: derived from a 20-carbon polyunsaturated fatty acid.

    Example: prostaglandin E₂ (PGE2) is responsible for fever, pain, and inflammation.

  5. Steroids

    Structure: four-fused carbon rings.

    Examples: cholesterol (a component of the cell membrane), estrogen (maintains the menstrual cycle), and testosterone (muscle growth, body hair, deep voice).
