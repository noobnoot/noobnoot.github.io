Introduction

Kinematics is the study of motion. Its roots can be traced back to the ancient Greeks, particularly Aristotle’s concept of motion.

What truly causes objects to move, and what keeps them in motion?

Views of Motion

We classify classical views of motion into two: (1) Aristotelian and (2) Galilean views of motion.

1. Aristotelian View of Motion

Aristotle explained motion based on observation and philosophical reasoning. His framework was qualitative rather than quantitative.

Key Features:

  1. Continuous Force—an object will only keep moving if force is continuously applied.

  2. Two Types of Motion:

    1. Natural Motion—inherent nature of objects
    2. Violent Motion—motion caused by an external force

  3. Free Fall—heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones.

2. Galilean View of Motion

While Aristotle’s ideas dominated scientific thought for nearly two thousand years, Galileo’s revolutionary perspective laid the foundation for modern physics.

Galileo Galilei, often called the “Father of Modern Science,” challenged Aristotle’s ideas through experiments and mathematical, quantitative reasoning.

Key Features:

  1. Inertia—objects in motion will move at constant speed unless acted upon by an external force.

  2. Forces Change Motion—a force is not required to keep an object moving, but to change its state of motion.

  3. Free Fall—all objects, regardless of weight, fall with the same acceleration without air resistance.
