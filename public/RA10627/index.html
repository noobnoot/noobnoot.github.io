Republic Act 10627

The Republic Act 10627, much known as the Anti-Bullying Act of 2013, is “an act requiring all elementary and secondary schools to adopt policies to prevent and address the acts of bullying in their institutions” (Official Gazette of the Republic of the Philippines, 2013). In the Implementing Rules and Regulations of the RA 10627, the Act now covers institutions that include private and public kindergarten schools and learning centers.

Why do we need the "Anti-Bullying Act of 2013" if schools can just devise their own policies to address bullying?

The Anti-Bullying Act of 2013 pairs up with the DepEd Order No. 40, S. 2012 or the Child Protection Policy in protecting and safeguarding the children and their rights against the adverse effects of bullying.

Section 1: The Anti-Bullying Act of 2013

The first section of the Republic Act of 10627 introduces and hereby proclaims the Act to be known as the "Anti-bullying Act of 2013" officially.

Section 2: Defining Bullying

In Section 2 of the Anti-Bullying Act of 2013, “bullying” is defined as:

  • “any severe or repeated use of one or more students of a written, verbal or electronic expression, or a physical act or gesture, or any combination thereof,
  • directed at another student that has the effect of actually causing or placing the latter in reasonable fear of physical or emotional harm or damage to his property;
  • creating a hostile environment at school for the other student;
  • infringing on the rights of the other student at school,
  • or materially and substantially disrupting the education process or the orderly operation of a school”

Definitions of Bullying

  • Cardona et. al. (2015) defines bullying as an act of repeated physical, verbal, social, or psychosocial aggressive behavior towards an individual or group.
  • UNICEF Canada (2012) defines bullying as “a serious form of violence against children which has a significant and lifelong physical and mental health effect upon the victim, as well as many other personal and social consequences for both the child victims and perpetrators.”
  • As an addition to the definition given by the Section 2, the IRR of RA 10627 also considers the following as an instance of "bullying":
    • Social bullying refers to any planned, repetitive, and hostile social behavior meant to harm others or belittle another individual or group.
    • Gender-based bullying is defined as any act that humiliates or excludes a person based on their gender identity or sexual orientation (SOGI).
    • Cyber-bullying refers to bullying done through the use of technology or any electronic means. This includes any conduct resulting in harassment, intimidation, or humiliation, such as but not limited to texting, email, instant messaging, chatting, internet, social media, online games, or other platforms or formats as specified in DepEd Order No. 40, S. 2012.

Acts of Bullying

Aside from the definition given in Section 2 of the Anti-Bullying Act of 2013, the Section 2 of the Act also considers the following as an act of “bullying”:

  • Any unwanted physical contact between the bully and the victim.
  • Any act that causes damage to a victim’s psyche and/or emotional well-being.
  • Any slanderous statement or accusation that causes the victim undue emotional distress.
  • Any act/form of cyberbullying (any bullying done through the use of technology or any electronic means) towards an individual.

Section 3: Schools toward Bullying

Section 3 of the RA 10627 covers the adoption of educational institutions toward the policies that address bullying. The policies shall include provisions that can be divided into eight subsections (3A-3H):

  1. Section 3A involves the prohibition of the following acts:
    1. Bullying on school grounds, including the properties adjacent to a school ground, or through the use of technology or an electronic device by the school is prohibited.
    2. Prevent any act or possibility that could put the victim in a hostile setting at school or could infringe the rights of the victim at school.
    3. Protect anyone who witnesses, reports, or offers information about bullying.

  2. Section 3B identifies the disciplinary actions that may be taken against a perpetrator for an act of bullying. In this section, the perpetrator of an act of bullying is mandated to undergo a rehabilitation program that will be administered by the institution concerned.
  3. Section 3C mandates institutions to establish clear procedures and strategies for:
    1. Reporting bullying or retaliation (“revenge” in layman’s terms) occurrences.
    2. Responding to and investigating reports of bullying or revenge as soon as possible.
    3. Restoring a victim's sense of safety and determining the student's need for protection.
    4. Protecting the person who reports a bullying incident.
    5. Providing counseling or referral for perpetrators, victims, and students' families.
  4. Section 3D stands to enable students to anonymously report bullying or retaliation. However, there will be no disciplinary action to be taken against the perpetrator.

  5. Section 3E covers false bullying accusations toward a student. It implements that the one who knowingly does the false accusation shall be subjected to disciplinary action.
  6. Section 3F enforces schools to educate students on the notion of bullying and the policies alongside it.
  7. Section 3G enforces schools to educate parents on the notion of bullying and the policies alongside it.
  8. Section 3H imposes schools to maintain a mandatory record of bullying or retaliation occurrences in the institution for the sole purpose of statistics. The information taken from the students shall be taken strictly confidential and only made available to school administration, teachers responsible for the said students, and the parents and guardians of those students.

Section 4: Addressing Bullying Policies

Schools are held accountable for putting in place and maintaining anti-bullying measures. Any instance of bullying or retribution witnessed by school personnel and administrators, students, parents, and volunteers should be immediately reported to the school administration or officers responsible. The school will investigate as soon as it receives the information. If the bullying has been confirmed, the school must take the steps outlined in the sections below:

  1. Notify law enforcement agencies if the culprit is facing a potential criminal charge under the Revised Penal Code.
  2. Take necessary administrative disciplinary action.
  3. Notify the perpetrator's parents or guardians.
  4. Encourage the victim's parents or guardians to inquire about the steps taken to prevent repeated bullying or retaliation.

Section 5: Reporting Requirements

This section maintains schools to mandate and implement anti-bullying policies provided that they should inform the school division superintendents. Additionally, at the beginning of each school year, schools shall submit all relevant information and statistics on acts of bullying and retaliation.

Section 6: Sanction for Noncompliance

If a school administrator fails to comply with the requirements of this act, the Secretary of the Department of Education shall pursue appropriate administrative actions.
