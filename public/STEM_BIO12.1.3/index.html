The Cell

Have you ever wondered how a small cut on your skin heals after just a few days? Or how a kitten grows bigger month after month?

These everyday events are possible because the cells in your body are constantly dividing and replacing old or damaged cells. The secret behind this process lies in the cell cycle.

The cell cycle is a series of events involving cell growth and division that produces two new daughter cells.

The cell cycle has two major stages: Interphase (where the cell grows and prepares) and M-phase (where the cell divides).

Interphase

Cells spend most of their time in interphase. It is where the cell is not dividing but is actively preparing for division.

The interphase is divided into three sub-stages:

  1. G1 Phase (Gap 1)

    The cell grows in size, produces proteins, and does its normal functions. A checkpoint here ensures conditions are suitable for the DNA replication.

  2. S Phase (Synthesis)

    The cell replicates its DNA, producing identical copies of chromosomes.

  3. G2 Phase (Gap 2)

    Further growth and preparation for cell division. Organelles and enzymes needed for division are produced. Final checkpoint ensures DNA replication is accurate.

If DNA is damaged or conditions aren't favorable, the cell enters a resting state. If the damage is too severe, they undergo apoptosis (programmed cell death).

M-phase

In this stage, the cell actively divides. It has two pathways:

  1. Mitosis

    Mitosis is the process by which a single cell divides to produce two genetically identical daughter cells. It is used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.

  2. Meiosis

    Meiosis is a special type of cell division in reproductive cells (e.g., sperm and egg). It reduces the chromosome number by half, producing four unique daughter cells, each with genetic variation.

For each pathways, the cell undergoes four phases: (1) prophase, (2) metaphase, (3) anaphase, and (4) telophase.

After mitosis or meiosis, the cell's cytoplasm divides into two distinct daughter cells in a final phase called cytokinesis.
