Introduction

Today, we can see pictures of Earth from space, but more than two thousand years ago, we already knew that Earth was spherical.

How did we figure out that the Earth was spherical before rockets, satellites, or telescopes?

The discovery of Earth's spherical shape dates back to the ancient Greeks.

Earth as a Sphere

The Greeks used careful observations and logical reasoning to prove Earth's spherical shape.

The Greeks' key observations and evidences:

  • Ships disappear as they sail away, as if going over a curved surface.
  • Earth's shadow cast on the Moon is always round during Lunar eclipses (see Figure 2).

These clues convinced Greek thinkers that Earth was not flat, but round like a ball—a sphere.

In fact, the shape of the Earth is an oblate spheroid.

Plato's Challenge

To the Greeks, observations are not enough to represent the “perfect” heaven that they believe in. Plato, one of their greatest philosophers, posed a challenge to astronomers:

How can we explain the motion of the heavenly bodies?

This was termed "Saving the Appearances," inspiring the following models that tried to organize the universe:

Astronomer Model Description
Eudoxus Geocentric
  • Introduced the first Earth-centered model.
Aristotle Geocentric
  • Refined Eudoxus' model
  • Heaven is perfect and unchanging
Aristarchus Heliocentric
  • Introduced the first Sun-centered model
  • Rejected due to lack of evidence
Ptolemy Geocentric
  • Proposed a detailed Earth-centered universe model in his work "Algamest"
  • The Church accepted his model, making it the dominant model for 1,400 years
Copernicus Heliocentric
  • Revived the Sun-centered model
  • Planets orbit around the sun on circular paths
Galileo Galilei Heliocentric
  • Defended Copernicus' heliocentric system against the Church
  • First to observe moons of Jupiter, Saturn's rings, phases of Venus, the moon's surface, Sunspots, and countless stars
Tycho Brahe Tychonian
  • Proposed geo-heliocentric model: Earth at the center, Sun and Moon orbit Earth, while other planets orbit the Sun
Johannes Kepler Heliocentric
  • Corrected Copernicus' model
  • Discovered that planets follow elliptical orbits

Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Tycho Brahe's model led Kepler to develop his laws: Laws of Planetary Motion.

  • Law of Ellipses—planets move around the Sun on elliptical orbits.

  • Law of Equal Areas—planet speed and distance are inversely proportional (see Figure 3).

    1. Perihelion—planet's position closest to the sun.
    2. Aphelion—planet's position farthest from the sun.

  • Law of Harmonies—orbital period ($T$) is directly proportional to its average distance ($R$) such that $T^2 = R^3$.
