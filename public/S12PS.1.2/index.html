The Origin of the Elements

Did you know that the calcium in your bones and the iron in your blood were made in exploding stars? This means that the elements in your body—carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, and more—once lived in the star’s core.

How were elements formed?

All the matter in the universe, from the smallest grain of sand to the largest stars, originated from elements formed in cosmic events through nucleosynthesis.

Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

In the beginning, there was a burst of extreme energy and a billion degrees Celsius of heat—the Big Bang. The universe soon expanded rapidly, filled with a thick cloud of subatomic particles like protons, neutrons, and electrons.

As the universe cooled just enough, protons and neutrons began to collide and stick together, forming the simplest, light elements like hydrogen, helium, and lithium. We call this process the Big Bang Nucleosynthesis.

Key Light Elements Formed:

  • Hydrogen (H-2, H-3)—the most abundant element
  • Helium (He-4)—the fusion of two hydrogen nuclei
  • Lithium (Li-6)—formed only in small amounts

Stellar Nucleosynthesis

As the universe aged, gravity pulled vast clouds of hydrogen and helium together, forming the first stars. Deep within the cores of these stars, temperatures reached millions of degrees, the perfect environment for nuclear fission.

Key Elements Formed:

  • Through Hydrogen fusion:
    • Hydrogen (H-2, H-3)
    • Helium (He-4)
  • Through Helium fusion:
    • Beryllium* (Be-8)
    • Carbon (C-12)
    • Oxygen (O)
    • Neon (Ne)
    • Magnesium (Mg) → Iron (Fe)

* Berylium-8 is a very unstable element. It immediately becomes Carbon-12 after formation.

Supernova Nucleosynthesis

When stars begin fusing iron, they gradually lose their internal energy. This makes the star weaker. When it can no longer support itself, it dies and explodes as a supernova.

This explosion is not just the death of a star. It’s the birthplace of the universe’s heaviest elements.

Key Heavy Elements Formed:

  • All elements heavier than iron (e.g, Gold (Au), Silver (Ag), Tin (Sn), and Polonium (Po)).

Note that supernovae do not make elements heavier than Uranium. Elements heavier than Uranium are generally synthetic, man-made elements.

Note that massive stars either become a neutron star or a black hole.
