The Cell

The human body is made up of many specialized cells, each with a specific role. For example, epithelial cells protect surfaces of the body, bone cells provide support, immune cells fight infection, and blood cells transport nutrients and oxygen.

Despite their diversity, all cells, from bacteria to humans, share basic features.

The cell is the fundamental unit of life.

Most cells are too small to see without a microscope. Scientists use light and electron microscopes, with the latter offering greater detail.

Discovery of Cells

The invention of the basic compound microscope by Hans and Zacharias Janssen in 1590 led to a series of discoveries by many scientists that shaped our understanding of cells and their role in all living organisms.

ScientistContribution
Robert HookeObserved cork tissue under the first compound microscope and coined the term “cell.”
Antonie van LeeuwenhoekThe first to observe living cells, such as bacteria and protozoa.
Matthias SchleidenProposed that all plants are made up of cells.
Theodor SchwannStated that all animals are also composed of cells.
Rudolf VirchowDeclared that all cells come from pre-existing cells.

Cell Theory

The discovery of the cell opened the gates for the Cell Theory—the fundamental principle in biology that explains the structure and function of all living organisms in terms of cells. There are two main theories.

  1. Classical Cell Theory

    Introduced by 19th-century scientists such as Schwann, this theory states that:

    1. All organisms are composed of one or more cells.
    2. The cell is the basic unit of life.
    3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

  2. Modern Cell Theory

    Advancements in molecular biology led to this theory, which includes the following additions:

    1. Energy flow occurs within cells.
    2. DNA is passed from one cell to another.
    3. All cells have the same basic chemical composition, regardless of species.

Prokaryotic Cells

A prokaryote is a simple, mostly single-celled (unicellular) organism that lacks a nucleus or any other membrane-bound organelle.

Most prokaryotes have a protective cell wall made of peptidoglycan, a long chain of carbohydrates. The cell wall maintains the cell shape and prevents it from dehydration, while the capsule helps it stick to surfaces.

Some prokaryotes also have flagella (for movement), pili (for sharing DNA during conjugation), and fimbriae (for attaching to host cells).

All bacteria and archaea are prokaryotic cells.

Their difference lies in their cell walls: bacteria use peptidoglycan, while archaea use pseudopeptidoglycan, helping them thrive in extreme environments.

Eukaryotic Cells

Unlike prokaryotic cells, eukaryotic cells have a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles (like the ER, Golgi apparatus, chloroplasts, and mitochondria). Organelles, meaning “little organs,” each have specific jobs, similar to how organs in the body function.

Eukaryotic cells are larger and more structurally complex than prokaryotes. Their outer is composed of a plasma membrane, a phospholipid bilayer with proteins.

All living things that are NOT bacteria or archaea are eukaryotic cells.

Eukaryotes include protists, fungi, plants, and animals. Unlike prokaryotes, eukaryotes have complex cellular structures that allow for compartmentalized functions. This internal organization supports more advanced processes like multicellularity, specialized tissues, and many ways of reproduction.

The Subcellular Organelles

Within each cell are specialized structures called organelles (meaning “little organs”) which carry out specific functions necessary for the cell’s survival and proper functioning.

Organelle Description
The following organelles are generally found in both animal and plant cells:
Nucleus Stores the cell's DNA and controls all cell functions, like growth and reproduction.
Plasma Membrane A semi-permeable membrane that controls the movement of substances in and out of the cell.
Cytoplasm Jelly-like fluid that fills the cell and holds organelles in place.
Mitochondria The powerhouse of the cell, producing ATP through cellular respiration.
Peroxisome Breaks down fatty acids and detoxifies harmful substances.
Endoplasmic Reticulum (Rough & Smooth) systems of canals that allow materials to pass from cytoplasm to nucleus.
  • Rough—contains ribosomes for protein synthesis
  • Smooth—processes lipids and hormones
Ribosomes Small structures that assemble amino acids into proteins.
Golgi Apparatus Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for delivery inside or outside the cell.
Cytoskeleton A network of microtubules and microfilaments that provide shape and movement.
  • Microtubules—slender, hollow, small tubes.
  • Microfilaments—slender, thread-like structures.
Organelles Found Mostly in Animal Cells:
Centrosome Organizes microtubules, which assist in cell division.
Lysosome Contains digestive enzymes that break down worn-out cell parts and invading pathogens.
Organelles Found Mostly in Plant Cells:
Cell Wall A rigid layer made of cellulose (or chitin for fungi and arthropod exoskeleton) that provides structure and protection
Chloroplasts Contain chlorophyll, a green pigment that captures sunlight to perform photosynthesis.
Central Vacuole Stores water, nutrients, and waste, and helps maintain the cell’s internal pressure.
