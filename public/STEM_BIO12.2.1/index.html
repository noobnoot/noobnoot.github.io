Introduction

Eating is part of your everyday life. The carbohydrates in your food are broken down into glucose, which your cells use as their main energy source. This notion connects directly to biochemistry.

Biochemistry is the branch of science that studies the chemical processes and substances that occur within living organisms.

Since our bodies are made up of billions of cells, and cells are built out of molecules, biochemistry focuses on these biological molecules—the ingredients of life.

Among these, a group of large molecules called the macromolecules plays the biggest role. These are:

  • Carbohydrates
  • Proteins
  • Lipids
  • Nucleic Acids

In this lesson, we deal with the simplest macromolecule, the carbohydrate.

In this lesson, you are expected to:

  1. Explain the biochemical process involving carbohydrates and describe the structure, types, and functions, highlighting glucose as the primary energy source of living organisms.

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids are made of polymers.

A polymer is a large molecule composed of many repeating smaller units called monomers.

For carbohydrates, the monomers (building blocks) are monosaccharides (also called "simple sugars").

Carbohydrates are the simplest biomolecules, often called "hydrates of carbon" because their composition follows a basic pattern: C, H, and O in the ratio 1:2:1 (or CH2O).

The most important of them is glucose, which is the primary energy source for all living cells. Other monosaccharides include fructose (from fruits and honey), and galactose (from milk).

When these simple sugars combine, they become:

  • Dissaccharides—two monosaccharides bonded together.
    • Sucrose—glucose + fructose—table sugar.
    • Lactose—glucose + galactose—milk sugar.
    • Maltose—glucose + glucose—digested starch.
  • Polyssacharides—many monosaccharides linked together.
    • Glycogen—the storage form of glucose in animals.
    • Starch—the storage form of glucose in plants.
    • Cellulose—a structural component of plant cell walls.
    • Chitin—exoskeletons of insects, crab, and shrimp, and cell walls of fungus.

Other unique monosaccharides include ribose and deoxyribose, which form the sugar backbone of RNA and DNA, respectively.
